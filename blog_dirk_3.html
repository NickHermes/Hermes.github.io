How multidisciplinary innovation teams tend to constrain radical innovation

  • posted by Nick Hermes
  • radical innovation, business, design

The core of your statement is that designers should launch viable (messy engagement) services instead of ideal (well-orchestrated) services.

Indeed, working/ designing and launching minimal viable products and services as part of an agile way of working is becoming an increasingly popular approach. Especially for service design, companies should adopt an iterative Scrum approach and thus stop with polishing and detailing, without really knowing their user’s needs.

However, the aim of (service-) designers should always be to make well-orchestrated products, through a minimum viable service approach, iterative way of working.

In order to become successful, an explicit design vision is necessary in order to create a holistic approach which is needed for the development of the product or service in Scrum (Boel, 2014). As Snelder et al. (2014) argues, the heterogeneity of the service interface gives rise to a need for orchestration in the design of services. Not only does a rock-solid vision align the scrum team, but it also ensures the service to maintain continual improvement once the first version is released.

Previous literature has shown that the level of control of human relations does not by itself determine the quality of a design (Snelders et al., 2014). Still, it could be argued that, sometimes, messy engagement is desirable, and the service should be specifically designed in such a way that it allows a certain degree of freedom for the user. However, if done properly, the demand for ‘messy engagement’ is actually an ‘ideal vision’. In other words, in this case the design of the service is perfectly orchestrated to perform in such a way that users experience freedom. From a designer’s perspective, you will still work towards an ideal situation of ‘messy engagement’.

Concluding, although designing for services should be an iterative process which involves launching minimum viable products, the core value should always be to strive towards an ideal, well-orchestrated service that fits the needs and demands of its target group

Striving towards an ideal, Interesting critique, for which I have one question: what is the role of vision in agile design and development methodologies? Aren't agile approaches seeking to learn along the way, not just about the problem area and the organisation of resources, but also about the goals itself? In design methodologies this seems to be the most important reason for wanting to iterate many times over: to let problem and solution spaces co-evolve into something new (Dorst, K., & Cross, N. (2001). Creativity in the design process: co-evolution of problem–solution. Design studies, 22(5), 425-437.) In that case, what is the use of a vision held constant over iterations?
