How multidisciplinary innovation teams tend to constrain radical innovation

  • posted by Nick Hermes
  • radical innovation, business, design

Although strategic designer should definitely work closely together with the whole innovation team, it should be the key role of the strategic designer to take the lead in providing inspiration, especially in the earlier stage of an innovation process. This is because, regarding radical innovation, there are quite some pitfalls for a designer to work closely together with a multidisciplinary innovation team that include marketeers, managers or engineers.

To begin with, designers often have a visionary perspective and approach towards solving problems. Marketeers on the other hand, tend to look at the past instead of the future, and look for rational, or ‘proven’ solutions based on current ‘rules’ of the market (Beverland, 2008). Even more so, as Beverland indicates, marketeers often favour incremental innovation, even when breakthrough innovation is required.

Likewise, managers within innovation teams tend to make decisions rationally, instead of intuitively. Thereby, innovation managers tend to favour incremental innovation over radical innovation and are constantly looking for decisions that are less likely to be risky and uncertain (Calabretta, Gemser & Wijnberg). This is augmented by the fact that a manager often perceives innovation from a monetary (risk versus benefit) perspective and thus prefers short-term returns (Christensen, Kaufman & Shih, 2010).

Furthermore, multidisciplinary innovation teams, especially when engineers are involved, often require comprehensive methods in order to communicate with each other (Jackson, 1996). Because of this, creative sessions and inspirational approaches may become more rational instead of intuitive. However, radical innovation often involves uncertainty, complexity, lack of information and time pressure, demonstrating the need for an intuitive decision making approach instead of a rigid rational or analytical approach (Calabretta, Gemser & Wijnberg).

As Beverland argues, strategic designers should be able to generate design-inspired innovations. Indeed, in order to create support amongst the innovation team, designers should create a communal understanding. But as multidisciplinary teams tend to constrain radical innovation, the strategic designer should set the basis for inspirational approaches in order to maximise a creative input in innovation and be able to think outside of the box. To conclude, the strategic designer should definitely involve all of the stakeholders and members of the innovation team, but the inspirational input should come from a design perspective.
